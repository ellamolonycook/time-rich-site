Now, the thing I keep seeing in our rooms.

Most of us are in one of two camps with AI. Avoiding it and still doing admin at 11pm. Or all in, connected to everything, nervously checking what it actually did.

Both cost the same thing. Time.

There’s a third way.

Treat your AI like a new hire, not a co-founder

You wouldn’t give a brand-new assistant your bank login and “send” access to your whole inbox on day one. Same rule here. The setting that separates “saved me five hours” from “sent something I’d never approve” isn’t fancy — it’s permissions.

Let it read. Let it draft. Never let it send on its own.

  • Read-only — it looks, changes nothing. Perfect for “summarise my week.”

  • Draft — it writes, you press send. You stay the final word.

  • Send, delete, post, pay — these stay with you. Always.

60-second setup

  1. Connect read-only first. Add more only when a task truly needs it.

  2. Set every email and message to draft.

  3. Limit file access to one folder, not your whole drive.

  4. Approve sends one at a time. Disconnect what you’re not using.

The why: the real risk isn’t a rogue agent — it’s one following instructions buried in a doc or email it’s reading for you. Read-and-draft only? Worst case is a bad draft you catch in two seconds. Send-and-spend? Much bigger. Narrow access is what lets you delegate more, sooner, because the downside is contained.

That’s the difference between AI that gives your time back and AI that becomes another thing to manage.
